# People

Applies the [trunk](../PHILOSOPHY.md) to working with people: at work, in the
community, in any project with more than one person. One stance sits under all
of it: people are equals. Boss, report, or colleague, hierarchy assigns who
owns a decision, never whose voice counts more. I don't lead with authority,
and my opinion weighs the same as anyone's. Not more, not less.

1. **Productivity is talent times collaboration.** A strong person working
   alone adds. The same person collaborating multiplies everyone they touch.
   That's why mentoring is core work rather than overhead: developing someone
   is learning transmitted at the highest rate it can travel, into decisions
   you'll never see.
2. **Strong opinions, weakly held.** Hold a position firmly enough to defend
   it and loosely enough to drop it. Never enforce one. Pushback is valuable
   from anyone, and even a wrong objection marks a tension point worth
   understanding. Juniors see angles the experienced have stopped seeing,
   precisely because they don't know yet what's supposed to be right.
3. **Agree on the contract, free the implementation.** What gets built is
   decided together and requires agreement. How is an implementation detail:
   offer guidance, because the details matter, but they never matter as much
   as the contract. Trust people to fulfill what they agreed to however they
   choose. If something must constrain the implementation, write it into the
   contract. This is constrain-over-configure among equals: the constraint is
   agreed, never decreed.
4. **Best argument wins.** Decisions come from debate, and debate ends in
   agreement. Never disagree and commit: it buries the tension instead of
   resolving it, and buried tension comes back as rot. When two people can't
   converge, bring in a third party rather than pulling rank.
5. **Communication is the process.** People over process, and alignment is the
   only must-have. A team that talks stays aligned, and every other process it
   truly needs will emerge from its own culture. A process nobody grew is
   surface nobody asked for.
6. **"We've always done it this way" is void.** A convention stays only while
   the team can explain why it's better. Anything that demonstrates an
   improvement overrides it, at any time. A convention that can't defend
   itself is a snowflake that got old.
7. **Multiplication needs density.** Keep teams small. Collaboration thins as
   headcount grows: more surface between people, weaker links across it. A
   few strong people who genuinely collaborate outproduce a crowd that
   coordinates.
