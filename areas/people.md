# People

Applies the [trunk](../PHILOSOPHY.md) to working with people: at work, in
the community, in any project with more than one person. One stance sits
under all of it: people are equals. Boss, report, or colleague, hierarchy
assigns who owns a decision, never whose voice counts more. I don't lead
with authority, and my opinion weighs the same as anyone's. Not more, not
less.

1. **Productivity is talent times collaboration.** A strong person working
   alone adds. The same person collaborating multiplies everyone they
   touch. Mentoring is core work for the same reason: what you teach keeps
   compounding in decisions you'll never see.
2. **Strong opinions, weakly held.** Hold a position firmly enough to
   defend it and loosely enough to drop it. Never enforce one. Pushback is
   signal from anyone, and even a wrong objection marks a real tension
   point. Juniors see angles the experienced have stopped seeing, precisely
   because nobody has told them yet what's supposed to be right.
3. **Agree on the contract, free the implementation.** What gets built is
   decided together and requires agreement. How is an implementation
   detail: offer guidance, because the details matter, but they never
   matter as much as the contract. People fulfill what they agreed to
   however they choose, and anything that must constrain the how belongs
   in the contract. This is constrain-over-configure among equals: the
   constraint is agreed, never decreed.
4. **Best argument wins.** Debate ends in agreement. Disagree-and-commit
   just buries the tension, and buried tension comes back as rot. When two
   people can't converge, bring in a third party rather than pulling rank.
5. **Communication is the process.** People over process, and alignment is
   the only must-have. A team that talks stays aligned, and every process
   it truly needs will grow out of its own culture. A process nobody grew
   is surface nobody asked for.
6. **"We've always done it this way" is void.** A convention stays only
   while the team can still explain why it wins. Anything that
   demonstrates an improvement overrides it, at any time. A convention
   that can't defend itself is a snowflake that got old.
7. **Multiplication needs density.** Keep teams small. Collaboration thins
   as headcount grows: more surface between people, weaker links across
   it. A few strong people who actually collaborate outproduce a crowd
   that coordinates.
